# Spa & Hot Tub Maintenance in the UAE Climate

A spa or hot tub is one of the most rewarding additions to a Gulf home or hospitality property, yet it is also one of the most demanding to keep in peak condition. The same conditions that make the UAE such a wonderful place to relax outdoors — relentless sun, high ambient temperatures and long humid summers — also accelerate water chemistry shifts, evaporation and equipment wear. A spa holds far less water than a pool, heats it to a higher temperature and recirculates it through a smaller body, so problems escalate quickly. The good news is that a disciplined routine, tuned to local conditions, keeps the water inviting and the plant room healthy all year round.

## Master Your Water Chemistry First

In a heated spa, warm water is a far more active chemical environment than a cool pool. Sanitiser is consumed faster, scale forms more readily and pH tends to drift upward, so testing two to three times a week is realistic in summer rather than the weekly check many owners assume. Aim to hold pH between 7.2 and 7.6, total alkalinity around 80–120 ppm, and a sanitiser residual appropriate to your system. UAE mains and tanker water is typically hard, so watch calcium hardness closely; high readings combined with high temperatures are the classic recipe for cloudy water and chalky scale on jets and headrests.

Because spa water is small in volume, doses are small too — always add chemicals gradually, with the circulation running, and retest before adding more. Overcorrection is the most common avoidable fault we see in the Gulf.

## Manage Heat, Evaporation and Refills

A 38–40°C spa sitting in a 45°C summer ambient loses water steadily to evaporation, and every refill dilutes your balanced chemistry and reintroduces hard, mineral-rich top-up water. A well-fitted insulated cover is the single most cost-effective piece of kit you can own here: it cuts evaporation dramatically, retains heat, keeps dust and grit out, and reduces the energy your heater burns through the cooler months. Inspect the cover for waterlogging and broken seals, as a sagging cover in this climate degrades fast.

During the hottest weeks, some owners actively cool rather than heat, since groundwater and ambient gains can push temperatures uncomfortably high. Whichever way you run it, top up little and often rather than letting levels crash, and rebalance after every significant refill.

## Keep Filtration and Surfaces Clean

High bather load in a compact volume means filters work hard. Rinse cartridge filters every couple of weeks and give them a proper deep soak monthly to clear oils, sunscreen and fine desert dust, which is far finer and more pervasive here than many newcomers expect. Keep a spare cartridge on hand so the spa is never out of service while one dries. Wipe the waterline regularly to stop body oils and dust forming a stubborn scum band, and clear skimmer baskets after any dusty or windy spell.

Plan a full drain and refresh roughly every three to four months, more often with heavy use. Fresh water resets accumulated dissolved solids that no amount of chemical dosing can remove.

## Protect the Equipment and Plant

Heat is hard on pumps, heaters, controllers and seals. Ensure the equipment area is shaded and ventilated so components are not cooking in direct sun, check unions and seals for the fine weeping leaks that scale quickly in hard water, and have pump and heater performance inspected seasonally. Catching a tired seal or a scaling heater element early is always cheaper than an emergency replacement in peak season.

A spa run on guesswork in this climate ages fast; one run on a steady, condition-aware routine stays a pleasure for years.
